# Scoring Rubric

Use this rubric to assign consistent, evidence-based scores for each criterion. Every score must map to observable behavior in the transcripts.

## Scale Reference

| Score | Label       | Meaning                                                       |
|-------|-------------|---------------------------------------------------------------|
| 9–10  | Excellent   | Consistently demonstrates this quality at a high level        |
| 7–8   | Strong      | Frequently demonstrates this quality with minor gaps          |
| 5–6   | Adequate    | Demonstrates this quality sometimes, room for improvement     |
| 3–4   | Developing  | Rarely demonstrates this quality, significant gaps            |
| 1–2   | Poor        | Almost never demonstrates this quality or counter-evidence    |

## Criterion 1: Credibility (1–10)

**What to look for:**

- Cites data, metrics, or specific examples when making claims
- References past experience or domain expertise appropriately
- Acknowledges uncertainty when unsure rather than bluffing
- Other participants defer to them or reference their input as authoritative
- Provides accurate technical or domain-specific information

**High score indicators (8–10):** Regularly supports arguments with evidence; other team members reference their expertise; corrects misinformation tactfully.

**Low score indicators (1–3):** Makes vague claims without support; contradicts themselves across meetings; other participants question their input.

## Criterion 2: Reliability (1–10)

**What to look for:**

- Reports completing action items they previously committed to
- Mentions of delivering work on time
- Other participants note their dependability (or lack thereof)
- Patterns of "I'll do X by Y" followed by evidence of completion in later meetings
- Missed commitments, delays, or excuses across meetings

**High score indicators (8–10):** Consistently reports completed work; others trust them with critical tasks; proactively updates the team on progress.

**Low score indicators (1–3):** Repeated mentions of incomplete tasks; others express frustration; pattern of rescheduling or missing deadlines.

## Criterion 3: Intimacy (1–10)

**What to look for:**

- Personal check-ins ("How are you?", "Hope your weekend was good")
- Empathetic responses to challenges ("I understand that's tough")
- Active listening signals (paraphrasing, asking follow-ups)
- Vulnerability or openness about their own challenges
- Supportive language when others present ideas or share problems
- Building personal connections beyond task-focused discussion

**High score indicators (8–10):** Regularly checks in on teammates; responds empathetically to challenges; creates psychological safety in meetings.

**Low score indicators (1–3):** Purely transactional communication; ignores emotional cues; dismissive of others' concerns.

## Criterion 4: Group Thinking (1–10)

**What to look for:**

- Builds on others' ideas ("Great point, and we could also...")
- Asks clarifying questions to deepen discussion
- Facilitates participation from quieter members
- Proposes compromises or synthesizes multiple viewpoints
- Avoids dominating the conversation; shares airtime
- Redirects off-topic discussions constructively

**High score indicators (8–10):** Actively synthesizes group input; encourages others to contribute; facilitates productive discussion; proposes solutions that incorporate multiple perspectives.

**Low score indicators (1–3):** Talks over others; ignores contributions; pushes their agenda without considering alternatives; disengaged from group discussion.

## Criterion 5: English Proficiency (1–10)

**What to look for:**

- Grammar accuracy in spoken contributions
- Vocabulary range and appropriateness for context
- Clarity of expression — can they articulate complex ideas?
- Fluency and natural flow of speech
- Use of filler words, false starts, or unclear phrasing
- Ability to adjust communication style for different audiences

**High score indicators (8–10):** Clear, articulate expression; varied vocabulary; communicates complex ideas effectively; minimal grammatical issues.

**Low score indicators (1–3):** Frequent grammatical errors that impede understanding; very limited vocabulary; struggles to express ideas clearly; heavy reliance on filler words.

**Important note:** This criterion evaluates communication effectiveness, not accent or dialect. Focus on clarity and comprehensibility.

## Writing Justifications

For each criterion, write a justification that:

1. States the score clearly
2. Cites 1–2 specific meetings by title or date
3. Describes the observable behavior that supports the score
4. Keeps the tone objective and professional

**Example:**
> **Credibility: 8/10** — In the "Sprint Planning - March 12" meeting, [Name] backed up their estimation with historical velocity data from the past three sprints. In "Design Review - March 18", they acknowledged uncertainty about a technical approach and proposed a spike to validate it, which the team appreciated.
